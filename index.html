Welcome to The Scarlett Box

The Scarlett Box is dedicated to ensuring menstrual dignity for women working at sea. We provide emergency sanitary boxes to women on ships who need quick access to essential sanitary products. Together, we can support health, dignity, and equality — even on the high seas.

Why Women Working on Ships Deserve Access to Free Menstrual Products

Women who work at sea as officers, engineers, and crew members play an increasingly vital role in the maritime industry. Their presence brings balance, skill, and perspective to a workforce that has historically been male-dominated. These women manage complex navigation systems, ensure vessel safety, lead teams, and handle demanding physical and mental tasks under challenging conditions. Yet, despite their professionalism and commitment, one essential aspect of their well-being is often overlooked — access to menstrual products.

Working on a ship is not like working on land. Once a vessel sets sail, crew members may spend weeks or even months at sea without access to traditional stores or medical facilities. In such isolated environments, the lack of immediate access to menstrual supplies can create anxiety, discomfort, and even health risks. Periods can be unpredictable, and without the right products available, women may be forced to improvise, which can lead to infections or distraction from critical duties. In a profession where focus and safety are paramount, this is unacceptable.

Providing free menstrual products for women officers is not a privilege; it is a necessity. Just as ships are required to carry first-aid kits, safety equipment, and food supplies for all crew members, menstrual products should be considered basic provisions. They are essential for maintaining hygiene, comfort, and dignity. Offering them free of charge ensures that women can perform their duties without fear of embarrassment or discomfort. It also sends a powerful message: that the maritime industry recognizes the unique needs of all its workers, regardless of gender.

Moreover, providing menstrual products promotes equality at sea. The maritime world has worked hard to recruit and retain more women, but inclusion goes beyond hiring — it means building an environment that supports their needs. Men do not face the same monthly biological challenges, and women should not be burdened with the additional stress of sourcing supplies while fulfilling the same demanding roles. Equal opportunity must include equal access to health and hygiene necessities.

Free menstrual products are also a matter of respect. They affirm that women’s health matters and that their contributions are valued. The maritime environment can be isolating and mentally taxing; something as simple as knowing that essential products are readily available can help reduce stress and create a sense of belonging. It shows that employers care not only about productivity but also about the human well-being of their crew.

Finally, making menstrual products freely available onboard is consistent with global movements toward menstrual equity. Around the world, workplaces, schools, and public spaces are beginning to understand that access to these products is a human right, not a luxury. By adopting this standard, the maritime sector can lead by example and demonstrate its commitment to fairness, dignity, and modern workplace values.

In short, women working on ships deserve free menstrual products because they are essential workers who face unique challenges. Supporting them is not only the right thing to do — it strengthens the industry as a whole by fostering a culture of respect, equality, and care on every voyage.
